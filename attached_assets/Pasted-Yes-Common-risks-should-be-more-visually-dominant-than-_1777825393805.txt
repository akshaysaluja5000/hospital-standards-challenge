Yes. Common risks should be more visually dominant than “what you’ll be expected to do.” Why? Because users act on threat and error faster than on abstract expectations, especially in compliance training. Right now your interface is telling them the safe/neutral content is primary and the high-liability content is secondary. That’s backwards.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
What’s wrong with the current hierarchy
The “What you’ll be expected to do” card is darker, higher on the page, and visually heavier, so it wins attention first. The “Common Risk Points” box has red text, but it sits lower, has less weight, and looks like an optional callout instead of the main danger zone.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
That means the user’s eye path is:
Page title
Tabs
Expected behaviors
Maybe risks
You want:
Page title
Top 3 common risks
What correct behavior looks like
Quiz / action
Best interface fix
Put risks first, not second
Move Common Risk Points above What You’ll Be Expected To Do. This is the biggest fix. Don’t overthink it.
Better section order:
Critical risk points
What surveyors expect
Study cards / quiz CTA
If you keep risks below, users will unconsciously treat them as supplemental.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
Split risk severity visually
Don’t show all risk bullets with the same weight. That’s lazy UI.
Use a tiered pattern:
Critical risk — red filled badge or tinted card
Frequent citation — amber/orange badge
Watch item — muted warning style
Even if everything is “important,” not everything is equally dangerous. If you flatten risk, users won’t know what to prioritize.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg

Specific UI changes I’d make
1. Make the risk container a filled surface, not just a red outline
Right now it looks too polite. Use:
dark red / burgundy tinted background
subtle glow or elevated surface
strong icon row at top
bold header like Frequent Survey Risks or Most Common Citations
Outlined boxes are weak. Filled surfaces command attention.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
2. Reduce the length of the risk bullets
Each risk line is too verbose. That kills scanability.
Instead of:
“Temperature logs showing only check marks with no documented corrective action when an excursion occurred.”
Do:
Missing corrective action on temp excursions
Blanket warmer above validated maximum
Unsecured oxygen cylinders
Extension cords used as permanent wiring
Expired PM stickers on active equipment
Then let users tap for explanation if needed.
Your current bullets are written like handbook prose, not interface copy.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
3. Add a risk count chip
At the top of the risk box:
5 common citation risks
or
5 high-risk failure points
This helps the section feel like a measurable object, not just a list.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
4. Use icon differentiation
Not every bullet needs the same red exclamation.
Try:
triangle alert for citation risk
flame/electric symbol for immediate safety hazard
clipboard/x for documentation failures
That makes the content chunk faster.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
5. Tie the CTA to risk
Your bottom CTA currently says:
Study Cards
Start Quiz
That’s generic.
Make the primary CTA risk-aware:
Quiz Me on These Risks
Practice High-Risk Scenarios
Test My Weak Spots
That aligns action with what just got highlighted.Screenshot-2026-05-03-111147.jpg
